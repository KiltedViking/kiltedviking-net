Swedish for railway is järnväg (eng. iron road), plural is järnvägar.

My grandfather was a stationmaster (at Vejbystrand and Helsingborg), my uncle worked for ASEA (now part of ABB - see Västerås below), I grew up below the Swedish West Coast Mainline, and my aunt and uncle lived by a mainline in Scotland. One could say I have trains in my blood. :-)

My train interests are mostly modern (diesel/electric), even though I enjoy visiting the odd 'steam gatherings' and historical railway societies.

Eskilstuna

Eskilstuna is partly known for the company Munktell (later Bolinder-Munktell, now part of Volvo Construction Equipment) who built one of Sweden's first steam engines: Förstlingen (eng. the Maiden or Bachelor, for lack of better translation).

The old railway company TGOJ, Trafikaktiebolaget Grängesberg-Oxelösunds Järnvägar (eng. Traffic Limited G-O Railways), (now part of Green Cargo, former Swedish State Railwys, SJ) has an engine shed in Eskilstuna. The old TGOJ transported iron ore from the iron ore fields in Bergslagen to the furnaces on the east coast (Oxelösund). In later days TGOJ has also been responsible for maintaining passenger coaches, but nowadays it's mainly transporting all kinds of goods (i.e. it's role in Swedish railways has changed a few times).

Eskilstuna is situated on the intersection of Svealandsbanan (Stockholm-Örebro) and the Västerås-Norrköping railway.

Läggesta and Mariefred

A historical train society runs narrow-gauge trains, steam and diesel, between Läggesta (on the main line, Svealandsbanan) and Mariefred (east of Eskilstuna).

For a historical approach to Mariefred, take the steam boat from Stockholm (arriving from the east).

Västerås

Västerås is home of ASEA, Allmänna Svenska Elektriska AB, (now part of ABB) who has built most of the Swedish electrical trains. In Helsingborg (south of Sweden, and where my uncle worked) the bogies for trains where worked on.
(In 1999 ABB sold, among other things, their train building business.)
