Introduction

History, or parts of it :-), is interesting according to me. I mostly enjoy 'visual history', i.e. ancient monuments, buildings, and other visual things that can be related to some historical event or person. Sometimes I like things because there is a related funny story or fact. Below are some places I recommend visiting.

Eskilstuna

Eskilstuna is a town of about 80.000 inhabitants situated at the southwest end of Lake Mälaren (about an hour west of Stockholm and where I have lived for the last 12 years). The town is named after St. Eskil, an English missionary (Eskil's Tuna, Tuna being the old name of Eskilstuna). (He was killed by some 'blokes' in 1080 in the town of Strängnäs, half an hour east of Eskilstuna.) Eskilstuna has for a long time been an industrial town and is today home to Volvo Construction Equipment (formerly Volvo BM and before that Bolinder-Munktell) and ASSA/Abloy. Other companies manufacture, and have manufactured, rifles, knives, spanners, files, and other tools.

Personally I find Eskilstuna to be somewhat of a 'dump', but then I have never liked towns (having grown up in a village with 3000 inhabitants). But this area has a lot of history (and lovely colours in the autumn) and I must say that Eskilstuna has 'cleaned up it's act' quite alot since I moved here in 1995! There are now a few festivals and other events, and the town has become more cultural (perhaps to be contributed to the arrival of the university?). It is also somewhat of a musical centre, producing quite a few successful musicians. And if you like shopping you will find something to do... and if you like watching caged animals, like white tigers, there is the Zoo (Parken Zoo).

Västerås

Västerås is slightly bigger than Eskilstuna and at the northwest end of Lake Mälaren (about an hour west of Stockholm and where I sometimes have had to travel to work). The town's name is derived from Western Aros (Västra Aros), where Eastern Aros was at the other end of Lake Mälaren. Västerås benefited from Eskilstuna not wanting the company ASEA (now ABB) to settle in Eskilstuna. Now (it seems like) every second building in Västerås has ABB written on it.
