Eskilstuna

Old Abbey and old Castle

In Eskilstuna there was an abbey, which was destroyed to build the castle, which in turn burnt down a few years later (instant karma? :-). Traces of these, or at least names associated with them, can be found at Castle Road (Slottsgatan) och Abbey Road (Klostergatan).

The old town and churches

The old town (east of the river) is worth a walk as is a look at the old Tunafors church (west of the river). The newer 'Abbey Church' (Klosters kyrka, north of the river) is built in the 20th centuary and is (I believe) mostly paid for by private donations. It was built at the north end of the main road through town (a road that now passes on the west side of the church, crossing the river over an even newer bridge [than the one that was there when the church was built :-)]).

Museums

Entry to Eskilstuna museums is free (at least when I wrote this) and well worth a visit! In the northwest part of the town centre is The Rademacher Smithies (Rademachersmedjorna), red two-storey wooden buildings where, among other things, pocketknives are made. Most of these smithies where moved from various locations in town and concentrated here to create this (working) museum.

Across the road, to the north, look at the old worker's houses (now private residences!) and maybe also the gardens of a factory owner (or manager?). In the gardens you will find quite a few different plants (and a nice place to have a picnic/coffee). Cross the bridge, north onto the islets (Faktoriholmarna), and cross the courtyard to enter the Eskilstuna's Cultural Museum which, among other things, shows how people lived here in the past and a full scale drawing of The Sigurd Carvings (see below).

Across the canal and draw bridge, and then the dam, you find the Munktell Museum which shows Volvo's past machinery. (Eskilstuna's art museum is supposed to be there somewhere as well... but I usually don't frequent it.)

Iron Age Forts

Around Eskilstuna there are quite a few iron age forts that are in very good condition, (at least) two of them within Eskilstuna and yet another one within walking distance (south of Vilsta). Heading north towards Sundbyholm there is a good one, The Vesta Fort (Vestaborgen), just west of Vallby (take road west towards Torshälla and then take road north towards Kumla, the second bigger one).

Sigurd Stone Carving and Sunbyholm's Castle

In Sundbyholm (north of Eskilstuna) is one of Sweden's largest stone carvings: The Sigurd Carving (Sigurdsristningen). It consists of one big carving telling the story of Sigurd, Fafne's Slayer (Sigurd Fafnesbane, i.e. Sigurd the slayer of the dragon Fafne). The woman that commisioned the carving, Sigrid, also had a bridge built across the river (now only a small stream) next to the carving. If in the area, this is not to be missed!

Sundbyholm also has a castle, or what I call an 'overgrown mansion'. It actually looks like a giant barn with windows from the outside. But passing through the doors it's more glamours, as is it's gardens. This is also a nice place to spend the day bathing in the summer (if you like lake water) and perhaps having a barbeque in the evening.

Jäder's Church and Fiholm Castle

Heading east from Sunbyholm there is a small road towards Kjula and Jäder. Jäder's Church is interesting because it's a big church in the middle of nowhere and with barely any parishioners. It was built by one of Sweden's bigwigs so that he could be buried in style. Just north is Fiholm Castle, which is interesting because only two of the four wings where built and there is only a big hole where the main house (refered to as a 'pigsty' by the builder) was supposed to be.
